N(ich)tThe starting point of the project N(ich)t is the observation that in everyday life, information and data about other people are continually being collectedŃboth consciously and unconsciously. From these fragmentary data, ideas about personality, identity, and character emerge. The project reverses this process by directing the act of data collection entirely toward the self and pushing it to its methodological limits.

Over several months, data were systematically gatheredŃanything that can be counted, measured, or documented. Bodily values, medical measurements, routines, possessions, sequences of time, spatial distances, repetitions and frequencies. There is no selection based on relevance, no weighting, no interpretation or hierarchy. Everything that can be recorded is collected.

The accumulated data ultimately condense into two books, one for each person. Both publications are defined by their large scope and high physical density. The design remains restrained and follows no narrative or interpretive structure. The content is presented factually, in its pure and immediate form.

At first glance, they create an impression of completeness. The sheer volume of information seems to provide a comprehensive picture of each person. Yet, the longer one engages with the material, the clearer a central void becomes. Despite the abundance of data, essential questions remain unanswered: How a person actually feels in a given moment. What meaning their experiences hold for them. What moves them internally. The books know a great deal about a person, and at the same time, know nothing of them.

This shifts the perception of knowledge itself. One begins to understand that knowledge and closeness are not the same, and that collecting information does not guarantee understanding. N(ich)t confronts us with the realization that even truthful, precise, and seemingly complete data cannot provide answers to what we intuitively perceive as essential. Everything in these books is correct, and yet what truly matters is missing. Essential questions cannot be answered in this way.

With continued examination, it becomes clear that the projectŐs limit does not lie in the amount of collected information. The collection does not fail due to a lack of data, but due to aspects of human existence that are inherently beyond complete capture. The more precisely things are measured, documented, and categorized, the more visible this boundary becomes.

Page by page, the books fill with precise information, yet they do not enable a full understanding of the individuals portrayed. A perceptible gap emerges between the quantity of available data and the actual comprehension of a human being. This gap is not a flaw or shortcoming of the work, but a central part of its message.

N(ich)t is not a portrait, not a staging, and not a self-description. It probes the limits of what can be captured. It is an exploration of the desire to make people comprehensibleŃand of the discomfort that arises upon realizing that this endeavor remains incomplete despite maximum precision. It describes all that cannot yield a complete conclusion about the whole. The project leaves no clear resolution, but rather a condition: What makes a person who they are will always lie outside the system that attempts to capture them.
